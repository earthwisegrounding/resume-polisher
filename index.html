TANK

CASE FILE: Psychological Manipulation / Identity Fraud

Investigating Agency: Internal Affairs, King County Sheriff's Office

Subject: Deputy Marcus "Tank" Washington, 38, African-American male, 6'4", 260 lbs

Charges (pending indictment): Official Misconduct, Assault (psychological), False Imprisonment via Coercion

Date of Initial Incident: March 2025 – ongoing pattern through February 2026

Tank wasn't just a cop. He was a fucking mirror that lied.

In the rain-slicked back lots of South King County—where the sodium lights buzz like dying wasps and the air smells of wet asphalt and diesel—he would pull over white guys in their mid-20s to early-30s. Clean-cut types. Tech bros, baristas with sleeve tattoos, frat leftovers who still wore backward caps on weekends. Always alone. Always at night.

He'd flash the lights once—short, professional. They'd pull over, hearts already hammering. Tank would step out slow, rain sheeting off the brim of his campaign hat, shoulders wide enough to block the headlights. Voice low, calm, almost kind.

"License and registration… and relax, brother. This ain't what you think."
— Body-cam audio, Incident #KC-25-0412, victim: Ethan Caldwell, 27

Then the turn. He'd lean close, rain dripping from his jaw, and murmur something only the driver could hear. Variations existed, but the core was always the same:

"You ever feel like there's someone else living inside your skin? Someone bigger. Darker. Someone who knows how to handle a baton, how to make a room go quiet just by walking in? Look at your hands, man. Really look. You sure those are yours? Because I swear I've seen them before… on me."

Most laughed nervously at first. Tank didn't. He'd hold up his own palm next to theirs—same lines, same calluses from years on the range—and whisper:

"See? Same scar on the thumb. Same little nick from that kitchen knife in '19. You're not Ethan. You're Tank. You've just been wearing the wrong face too long. The department needs you back on duty… but first you gotta remember who you really are."

Some folded fast. Others fought it—screaming, crying, begging for proof. Tank had proof. Photos on his phone: the same guy, but blackface-level makeup, same jawline, same eyes. Doctored? Of course. But in the dark, with adrenaline and fear, it looked real enough. He'd show dashcam stills of "himself" making arrests—same car, same badge number. Same hands gripping the wheel.

He never touched them sexually. That wasn't the point. The point was erosion. Make them doubt their own reflection until they started talking like him. Walking like him. Calling dispatch in the middle of the night asking for backup because "they" were coming for the white mask.

"I woke up this morning and my skin felt… wrong. Too pale. Like I'd been bleached. I looked in the mirror and Tank was staring back. He told me to report for shift. I almost did."
— Victim impact statement, Ryan Holt, 29 (hospitalized for dissociative episode, March 2026)

IA got wind when one victim—former Marine, no history of mental illness—showed up at the precinct in full uniform he'd bought online, demanding to be "relieved of white duty." He had Tank's badge number tattooed on his forearm in fresh ink.

Tank sits in holding now. Smiling. Says it's all a misunderstanding. Says the victims are just finally waking up.

But late at night, when the fluorescent lights in the jail block flicker, guards swear they hear two voices coming from his cell.

One deep. One scared.

Both saying the same thing:

"I'm Tank."
